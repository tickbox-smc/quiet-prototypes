About Quiet

Who built this, why, and where it's at.

Who built this

I'm Chris Falck. I've spent my career building and integrating systems at scale. Quiet is the first time I've aimed that at a problem I've been living with personally.

The first thing I built for the internet was an email auto-responder. A product that, as it turned out, became a vehicle for spam and had to be closed down. I've spent the last twenty-odd years on the other side of that problem.

The email overload problem is one I share with most of the professionals I know. The email scam problem is one I've watched affect people I love. Both felt like problems worth solving properly.

Where Quiet Protect came from

Quiet Protect started with a question about an email.

My partner's father is in his eighties. He's independent, capable, and sharp enough to forward a suspicious email with a note saying "this looks a bit weird, doesn't it?" He was right. The email was a scam. Convincing logo, plausible language, the right sense of official urgency. And he'd spotted something was off, but couldn't quite be sure.

"I've just received this, it looks a bit weird."

Knowing something felt wrong but not being certain. Having to forward an email to a family member just to check. That is exactly what Quiet Protect is designed to prevent. Not by taking away independence or making someone feel managed. By removing the doubt before it arrives. The email simply doesn't reach the inbox. The ones that do are safe.

The scams targeting older adults are getting better. The impersonation of government bodies, banks, and trusted services is increasingly convincing. Existing email clients offer no meaningful protection and spam filters catch bulk mail, not carefully crafted fraud. There is no product on the market that gives a family member visibility of what's reaching a vulnerable adult's inbox, while keeping that inbox private. That gap is what Quiet Protect addresses.

Where Quiet came from

The core Quiet product, the inbox replacement for overwhelmed professionals, is the problem I've been living with for years. Email has become the default coordination layer for work, which means it has also become the default source of interruption, anxiety, and missed things that didn't actually matter.

Every tool in this space makes the inbox better to deal with. Quiet is built on a different premise: the inbox shouldn't be something you deal with at all. The AI does the reasoning. You see what needs your attention, what's been handled, and what's been quietly suppressed. You never open your email client again.

Both products share the same philosophy: email should work quietly in the background, not occupy the foreground of your day.

What stage this is at

This is pre-commercial. There are no paying users, no live accounts, no terms of service. What exists is:

What's built

Complete Click-through prototypes: Quiet and Quiet Protect, web and mobile
Complete Microsoft Graph API integration: live M365 connection and OAuth
Complete Quiet Protect scoring engine: calibrated stable across 60 regression runs
Complete Quiet scoring engine: calibrated and stable; combined threat and importance scoring
Complete Email processing pipeline: fetches, scores, and routes live email
Complete Live demo: Quiet Protect running on a real Microsoft 365 inbox
In progress Quiet Protect notification layer: alert emails to the family manager
Later Gmail and IMAP support, attachment scanning, multi-user accounts

The scoring engine has been through 18 prompt iterations and a structured calibration process across 20 test cases: obvious scams, safe emails, boundary cases, false positive candidates, and deliberate edge cases. Three full regression runs. Sixty from sixty routing decisions correct. That is the part of the system that required the most rigour, and it is done.

The pipeline that connects it to a live inbox is also done. The demo at demo.getquiet.co.uk is running against a real Microsoft 365 mailbox with the scoring engine active.
